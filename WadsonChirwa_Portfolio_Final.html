Policy Brief  ·  SRHR  ·  Zomba District  ·  2025

Strengthening Adolescent SRHR Services Through Teen Clubs in Zomba District, Malawi

Wadson C. Chirwa  |  Zomba District Health Office  |  Ministry of Health, Malawi

The Problem

Adolescents aged 10–19 make up around 22% of Malawi's population, yet they face high rates of unintended pregnancy, HIV infection, and unsafe abortion. In Zomba District, adolescent pregnancies are a leading cause of school dropout among girls, and modern contraceptive use remains below 30%. The main barriers are stigma, poor provider attitudes, distance to facilities, and lack of youth-friendly services.

The Evidence

  • The Malawi DHS (2020) shows 29% of girls aged 15–19 have already started having children.
  • WHO (2023) finds that peer-led SRHR education can reduce adolescent pregnancy by up to 35% in sub-Saharan Africa.
  • Teen Clubs at Matawale, Domasi, Mambo, Thondwe, Chilipa, and Bimbi show higher SRHR knowledge and contraceptive uptake among participants versus non-participants.
  • Digital outreach via WhatsApp and Facebook effectively reaches out-of-school youth in low-resource settings (UNFPA 2022).

Policy Options

  • Option A: Expand Teen Clubs to all health facilities in Zomba District (currently 6 facilities).
  • Option B: Integrate SRHR digital outreach into existing HSA WhatsApp and community platforms.
  • Option C: Train and deploy peer health educators in schools and communities alongside facility-based Teen Clubs.

Recommendation

We recommend combining all three options: expand Teen Clubs to every facility, integrate SRHR messaging into HSA digital platforms, and deploy trained peer educators in schools. This multi-pronged approach tackles both facility and community barriers simultaneously. The cost is low relative to impact, and the existing district workforce — HSAs, CHAGs, and Teen Club mentors — can deliver this without major new resources.

Evidence Brief  ·  Adolescent SRHR  ·  2025

Peer-Led SRHR Interventions for Adolescents in Low-Resource Settings: Evidence and Implications for Zomba District, Malawi

Wadson C. Chirwa  |  Zomba District Health Office  |  2025

Background

Adolescents in sub-Saharan Africa face serious barriers to sexual and reproductive health services — including stigma, provider attitudes, cost, and distance to facilities. In Malawi, where 22% of the population is aged 10–19, adolescent pregnancy and HIV infection remain persistent public health challenges. District programmes such as Teen Clubs provide a platform for peer-led SRHR education but are often under-evaluated and insufficiently resourced.

Evidence Review

A review of published literature and programme data from 2015–2024 shows strong evidence that peer education improves adolescent SRHR outcomes. Denno et al. (2015) found peer-led interventions increased condom use by 23% and delayed sexual debut. WHO (2023) recommends youth-friendly models — including peer education, community outreach, and digital health — as core components of adolescent SRHR programmes in LMICs. Chandra-Mouli et al. (2020) found that multi-component programmes are more effective than single-component approaches in increasing adolescent contraceptive uptake. Locally, Teen Club data from Zomba District (2023–2024) shows participants have higher SRHR knowledge scores and are more likely to seek family planning services.

Implications for Practice

The evidence strongly supports scaling peer-led SRHR programmes in Zomba District. Key actions: (1) Expand Teen Clubs beyond the current six facilities to all district health facilities. (2) Provide structured training, supervision, and refreshers to maintain peer educator quality. (3) Use WhatsApp and SMS to reach out-of-school youth. (4) Integrate pre/post knowledge assessments into all Teen Club sessions to build evidence and drive continuous improvement.

Conclusion

Both global evidence and local programme experience in Zomba confirm that peer-led, multi-component SRHR interventions are effective, practical, and cost-efficient in low-resource district settings. Strengthening Teen Clubs through systematic mentorship, digital integration, and routine evaluation is a high-value investment in adolescent health — directly supporting Malawi's national SRHR targets.

Event-Based Surveillance (EBS) Protocol — Zomba District

System design, operational summary, and key indicators for the Zomba District EBS programme.

How Signals Are Detected and Acted On

1
Detection

Community, facility staff, media scanning, and health hotline reports

2
Triage

Signal logged and classified: immediate / 24h / 48h priority

3
Verification

IDSR Coordinator contacts source; lab samples collected if needed

4
Risk Assessment

Assess likelihood, severity, and spread; escalate if threshold met

5
Response

Rapid response team deployed; outbreak investigation started

6
Reporting

Situation reports submitted; feedback bulletins shared with all facilities

The Four EBS Streams

Community EBS: HSAs and CHAGs report unusual health events by phone or form to the district EBS focal person.

Facility EBS: Clinicians and lab staff report unusual syndromes, case clusters, or unexpected deaths using a daily checklist.

Media Scanning: Daily review of national news, social media, and radio for potential health threats.

Hotline EBS: Community members call the district health hotline; trained Health Hotline Workers triage each call.

What Makes This System Work

Multi-stream approach captures signals from community, facility, media, and hotline at the same time.
Structured triage ensures urgent signals are escalated within 24 hours, not lost in paperwork.
DHIS2 dashboards allow real-time monitoring of reporting completeness and disease trends.
Regular supervision and weekly feedback drove reporting completeness from 71% to 94% in two years.
